<p>We were walking around a Russian city, something between Petersburg and Moscow. It was a warm summer day. A grand mass event was being set up downtown. My sister and I decided to go closer and see what it would be.On a body of water — something between the Neva and Chistye Prudy — an enormous, ornate three-story Russian log house-raft was moored at the pier, like something out of a museum of wooden architecture in Novgorod, except built from bright timber just freshly coated in yacht varnish. My sister and I climbed up onto the log house (or into it?). It was cramped and crowded. Inside had gathered the curious onlookers you often run into on your way to the main square on City Day, Navy Day, Victory Day, and so on.Our vessel began sailing out to the middle of the body of water. When the house-raft reached the center of the "lake," from the porch, refitted as a slipway, they lowered a vessel that looked like a submarine. It turned out the event was dedicated to the ceremonial launch of a new secret submarine. We stood on the balcony above the porch, a bit to the side, and watched as, from the porch, sailors in dress uniform started climbing down into the submarine, disappearing one by one into the dark-blue hatch jutting out over the water's surface.When the action started, all the "passengers," the onlookers, began moving — some running, some walking — toward the porch on the front side of the house, to get a better look at the submarine. The house started listing to one side. The passengers were no longer walking but sliding toward the front of the house. As a result the raft tilted, like that scene in Titanic when Leo and Kate were going under the water. At that moment I realized things were bad, and I jumped as far as I could off the balcony, diving into the water headfirst. I surfaced and saw the log house flipping over, flooding and burying the submarine under its mass. People were jumping off in every direction — from the porch, the balconies, the railings. Chaos broke out — people, ribbons, sailors' caps, and a terrifying silence. I dove a little deeper, grabbed the first child I could reach, and swam with everything I had toward the shore, shouting for my sister the whole time. I spotted Nastya. It was about a hundred meters to the shore. The water was murky and cold.We got out onto the shore. People were gathering. Cars were pulling up. I ran as far from the shore as I could toward the houses, where it seemed to me we might find help...</p>
