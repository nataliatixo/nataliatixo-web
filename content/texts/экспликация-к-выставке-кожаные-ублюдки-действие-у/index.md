<p><strong>ANTONOV GALLERY</strong></p>

<p>LEATHER BASTARDS.</p>

<p>Part one, "ACTION U"</p>

<p>Artists: Sergei Bondarev, Tanya Kolpakova, Andrei Pakhomov</p>

<p>Curator: Andrei Andreev</p>

<p>Text: Natalia Tikhonova</p>

<p>Through the example of artistic practices, the exhibition series "Leather Bastards" proposes methods for studying the 'body' as a boundary and as a frame — one that restrains, but also defines, self-identification.</p>

<p>'Leather bastards' is a meme, a phrase from videos about the robots of 'Boston Dynamics,' which is what they call the humans they encounter along their path. In contrast to Gilles Deleuze's concept of the 'body without organs,' which calls for shedding the materiality and boundedness of the body, in the machine's representation a human being is an interface, a skin — that is, an external organ or outer covering.</p>

<p>Today the concept of the 'body' as ephemeral virtual matter has been realized in digital reality. Online, a person, constructing their identity out of images and thoughts, blends 'themselves' with other virtual embodiments and information streams. This uncertainty — together with the loss of corporeality and the alienation of the spiritual from the physical — provokes frustration, anxiety, and depression: in the gray zone between online and offline we need our own materiality, tactility, and geography. The dividing line between the 'human' and the 'non-human' sits on the surface of the flesh — a shell that restricts and censors, while at the same time allowing one to determine one's own 'self.'</p>

<p>In the exhibition "Action U," the first in the "Leather Bastards" series, a theatrical scene of the body's destruction unfolds inside the gallery. The visual means used, in their very objecthood, present exhibits — physical traces of a body whose presence is registered through its loss. The three artists destroy, alter, dissolve, and hide bodies using various artistic constructs: the tactility of painting, the decorative formality of collage, and the form-making of textile.</p>

<p>Andrei Pakhomov's torsos are shells whose contents destroy themselves from within. The artist dissolves, decomposes, erases the figure, revealing matter in the flickering gaps of paint. Color, light, and body smolder and go out on the plane of the canvas. Even though the figures are fragmented and ghostly, their reality is felt through recognizing the candor of the painting. The submedial space of the picture acts on different levels of perception: contextual, visual, sensory, perceptual. The affects that arise from the viewer's experience, anchoring physical presence in the gallery space, attest to the materiality of the depicted, bodiless body.</p>

<p>In Sergei Bondarev's collages, the human figure is altered and deformed according to the demands of pop culture. Where Cindy Sherman and Vladislav Mamyshev-Monroe created self-portraits to reclaim their own bodies by presenting them, Sergei Bondarev works with an alienated image, using the practice of objectification as an artistic method, turning the face into a flat material. In Tanya's textile objects, the loss of the body is shown quite literally: the art objects present the absence of flesh. Traditionally gender-marked women's clothing — the dress, usually made to hide, suppress, and censor the body — brings anthropomorphic schemas of feminine imagery to the foreground.</p>

<p>The objects in the exhibition testify to the destruction, the loss, of bodies, while at the same time registering the bodily presence of the viewers. Taking up the maximum possible area across the gallery's horizontal plane, the objects obstruct movement and cause discomfort. Visitors' bodies find themselves constrained by the art objects.</p>

<p><strong>Sergei Bondarev</strong> is a multidisciplinary artist who works with painting, sculpture, textile, and theatrical projects. Combining and rethinking classical painting techniques, the aesthetics of mass culture, pop art, and fashion, he uses vivid, theatricalized expression and the coloristic possibilities of material to create his own sensual images and expressive language.</p>

<p><strong>Tanya Kolpakova</strong> is an artist, designer, gallerist, curator, and teacher of the history and theory of fashion, a designer of museum and gallery displays; as a costume designer she has worked in theater, film, and television. In her projects related to fashion and contemporary dance, Tanya explores the interaction between body and clothing. Using laconic strokes, blots, and lines, and working with the dress as a pictorial plane, she expresses images of surrounding reality in textile, in minimalist constructivist forms.</p>

<p><strong>Andrei Pakhomov</strong> is an artist, graphic artist, and co-founder of the "Rare Book from Saint Petersburg" publishing house. A recognized master of graphic art and mentor to several generations of important Petersburg artists, he has reworked academic traditions and experimented with the possibilities of visual means — line, blot, color, texture, compositional canons. The monochrome canvases with portraits, torsos, and body fragments that the artist has often turned to sit on the border of "the Leningrad late-modernist tradition and contemporary art."</p>
