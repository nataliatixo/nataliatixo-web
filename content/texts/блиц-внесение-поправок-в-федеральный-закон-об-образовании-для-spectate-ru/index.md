<p><em>artist, curator</em></p>

<p>Harry Potter and the Order of the Phoenix, 2007, directed by David Yates</p>

<p>I am an artist and curator, so educational activity is part of my practice. I take part in building the educational project "Natural Circulation," sometimes teach at the Paideia school, and give lectures, reading groups, and workshops at various institutions. Together with Marina Pugina I'm working on research devoted to self-organized schools of contemporary art and how power is distributed inside such schools. It's no secret that lectures, master classes, and workshops are sometimes the only source of income for "non-gallery" artists — those who don't produce object-based art. My exhibitions often take the form of lecture-performances, I run a Telegram channel, write texts... In short, it turns out that almost any event, activity, or undertaking in the field of contemporary art can be called an educational or enlightening practice in the broad sense of the word — whether you're making media, a website or a blog, writing articles, giving master classes, or leading a tour of your own exhibition. That's exactly why the law regulating educational activity provoked such a wide response in the contemporary art community and prompted an open <a href="https://docs.google.com/document/d/1g1jywnO8bpLngs6fqjp-TRtfiFbXzCDUQ3gaqdI-WfA/edit">collective letter</a> against its adoption.</p>

<p>Pedagogical practices are an important strategy in contemporary art, from modernist schools and amateur circles all the way to today's media-activist and archival projects. Thanks to how simple they are to organize communication around, and the diversity of their structures, schools, lessons, and educational projects have become an effective, accessible, and widespread form of activist and artistic practice — which is why artists at the start of the 21st century, as part of the "social turn," turned <a href="https://symposium.curatorialforum.art/glossary-educationaly-turn">to pedagogical projects</a>. It's clear that enlightenment, education, and pedagogy are important processes that shape communities, ties, and civil society.</p>

<p>The law regulating enlightenment activity is a logical step given our country's current state policy. In a modern totalitarian state, the sphere of education and enlightenment can only exist under conditions of strict regulation and censorship. To see just how much pedagogical projects fall within the state's sphere of interest, one need only recall, for example, the case of <a href="https://www.svoboda.org/a/30884440.html">Mikhail Iosilevich</a>, opened over classes training election observers run by the "<a href="https://www.golosinfo.org/">Golos</a>" movement.</p>

<p>Honestly, I don't know how this law will work in our conditions, but as the open letter says — for many it's obvious that it will be repressive in character. That much is suggested by the timing chosen for its adoption, the haste, the lack of proper drafting, and the vagueness of its wording. We saw a similar story with the "foreign agents" law, which has already claimed a victim in artist Daria Apakhonchich, who, incidentally, was teaching Russian as a foreign language.</p>

<p>I'm not sure the collective letter will help, but it's an important claim to visibility for the art community. What matters is that a community of professionals of different ages, geographies, and contexts has now formed within Russian contemporary art — people ready to unite, collaborate, and stand up for their interests, rather than carry on endless internal disputes over discourse and fights over "resources" (really, over their remnants).</p>

<p>What I find really appealing is both the letter's scale and its professional language. It isn't a formalized "scream" or affective outrage, but an analysis of the law done with a lawyer, plus substantial work with the media to generate a public response. For all of us this became another important experience of activist work and real politics. More and more people in the art world are ready to take responsibility and speak openly about their positions, to shed the harmful habit of "not sticking your neck out," to assert their rights, to be visible not only within institutional and genre frameworks, but to act and engage in activism and politics in the broad sense of the word.</p>

<p>I believe that active participation in political processes will soon become as much a commonplace as, say, feminism — moving from a niche activity for a narrow circle of people into the sphere of everyday life, like hygiene or regular exercise. Sometimes it's boring, and even unpleasant in places, but it's necessary — the way going to the clinic or dealing with your taxes is necessary. At the same time, "activism" can look completely different — from collective letters like this one, to showing up on the streets, media activism, working with NGOs, volunteering, exercising your electoral rights, working at elections, forming mutual-aid groups, and so on. We need to get involved and learn in practice how political processes work, and to build the state institutions that we lack. NGOs can't run on donations alone — not unless we're talking about sums comparable to the budgets of certain government agencies. Only joint, mass action and the involvement of a large number of people can be commensurate with the machinery of repression.</p>

<p>Of course, one could say that letters like this change nothing, just as expressing your civic position through solo pickets or rallies, working at elections, direct financial help, or media activism change nothing... I often slip into a feeling that everything I do is meaningless, but if you keep devaluing what you do, how can you do anything at all — whether that's art or activism?</p>

<p>First, it's new knowledge and experience, a new skill, a way out of your own "bubble." Second, by sharing our experience and resources, we can find support, or give it to someone else. Along the way we talk, make friends, help each other. Sometimes it's simply nice to get a result by solving someone else's problem.</p>

<p>I was recently struck by the words of artist and activist <a href="http://aroundart.org/2018/01/24/anya-kurbatova/">Anya Kurbatova</a>: when she served as a member of the <a href="http://onkspb.ru/">ONK (Public Monitoring Commission)</a>, often the only thing she could do was stand there and argue with pre-trial detention and prison service staff. It didn't help, but as long as she stood there yelling, they didn't beat the detainee. I have a feeling that for the foreseeable future, all our actions are going to follow a similar logic.</p>
