<p>Text from the article:</p>

<p>The "Video Art Club" at the Kurekhin Center for Contemporary Art was launched in August 2020 by curators Natalia Tikhonova and Natasha Trembovetskaya. Ahead of the club's 7th meeting — the meetings happen once a month in the video hall — ART Uzel asked its creators how and why all of this works, how much longer it will go on, and what kind of viewer this community is waiting for.</p>

<p><em>Interviewed by: Anna Krylova</em></p>

<p><strong>How did the idea to create the club come about?</strong></p>

<p><em>Natalia Tikhonova:</em>During quarantine there were no exhibitions, and people spent a lot of time at home. When we got together we often watched clips, videos, and our own phone-shot sketches, and discussed them. It seemed to us then that this was a great format — watching together with discussion. We realized there was no place in the city where you could watch video in this mode, or really show and discuss video art at all. We got interested in organizing an event like that.</p>

<p><em>Natasha Trembovetskaya:</em>Yes, the idea for an event grew out of those kitchen gatherings — one that would, at its core, preserve and carry over the informality of watching at home, while adding to it a chorus of different viewpoints, a discussion. At first we had no restrictions on video format. Natasha and I watched all kinds of genres — from popular clips and TikToks to experimental video and video art. At some point we realized that our amateur interest in the moving image could become a club format and a long-running curatorial project.</p>

<p><em>Natalia:</em>Natasha had done a video art exhibition in the catacombs of the Petrikirche in Saint Petersburg, and a lot of my curatorial projects work in the format of events, meetings, and creating a place for discussion. Many of my curatorial projects are focused on process. So our two poles came together — to make a combination of a discussion club about the contemporary genre boundaries of video art, and a classic screening.</p>

<p>We decided to pitch this project to the Kurekhin Center, because we have a curator there who's friendly to us, Katya Yushkevich. We wrote to her, they liked the idea, we quickly agreed on the details, wrote the necessary texts to launch the project, and put out an open call.</p>

<p><em>Natasha:</em>To our surprise and unqualified delight, we received more than 30 video works within two weeks. It turned out that many artists, directors, videographers, and just people who shoot video wanted to show and discuss their work. Of course, that level of demand made a strong impression on us as curators. It became clear that we were setting in motion some new (or simply curatorially under-attended) field of contemporary experimental video art. Venues where you can watch video in a screening format, with discussion on top of that, are few or simply don't exist, while there's a lot of content out there that could be considered video art.</p>

<p><em>Natalia:</em>I feel like we caught some important wave. For example, artist Diana Galimzyanova took part in our very first screening, and by autumn she'd started her own Video Club in Moscow at Bomba gallery. Video is rarely shown in Russia, especially if you're shooting something experimental that hasn't yet been shaped into exhibition projects. On the other hand, today cinema, video, and moving-image art have reached a turning point. Now everyone can shoot and edit. Even on a phone you can shoot and edit a finished video or film in good quality. There are a lot of accessible editing tools available. This is changing the film industry and video art, and, more than that, the logic of perception itself. We consume and produce video content on YouTube, Instagram, TikTok. Users are tuned in to video content, and it's easy to make it yourself.</p>

<p><em>Natasha:</em>Besides the video content itself, which is the sense-making factor behind the club's existence, I think it's important to mention the performative character of these screenings. Video art changes how you perceive space. Not least, it's a performative story — the interaction between the hosts (Natasha and me) and the audience, interactions between viewers, and generally the complex, multi-layered communication inside the Kurekhin Center's "black box." Video is the initiating element that accumulates and drives the communication. And Natasha and I don't just watch/discuss video — we watch how the space changes under the influence of the moving image. That's the mesmerizing side of the club.</p>

<p><strong>Do you have any selection criteria? Can any video make it into a screening?</strong></p>

<p><em>Natasha:</em>The Video Art Club's main task is a joint search — together with viewers and creators — for the boundaries of the video art genre. Such a search is inherently experimental and genre-crossing. We take every video that's sent to us. We deliberately don't rate things — quality content/low-quality content, bad/good. We try to build a living dialogue, and within that dialogue clarify for ourselves the main tendencies and aspirations of authors working with video.</p>

<p><em>Natalia:</em>The project's DNA is not to select video. Our goal is to gather different genres and approaches, show them, and in that way archive them. We're limited by runtime — from 42 seconds to 42 minutes. So far the longest video that's taken part in a screening is "The Divine Matrix" by the group TO Nadenka, which ran 30 minutes. When putting together a screening, we try to make the videos work complementarily, so they complete each other. Sometimes guests don't understand why we're watching certain things at all. But that's part of the process — sometimes the videos are contentious, hard to watch, or use commercial, advertising-style techniques. In that case, we focus on the discussion that opens up around them. We don't rate or select — what matters to us is representing diversity.</p>

<p><em>Natasha:</em>Each screening has 9-10 participants, but one day maybe we'll do just 2 participants. Our open call is endless. Send your work to videoartlclubrun@gmail.com!</p>

<p><strong>Do you build a narrative? What's the organizing principle of a screening?</strong></p>

<p><em>Natalia:</em>Natasha and I put together screenings by turns. I have one principle, Natasha has another.</p>

<p><em>Natasha</em>: Of course we're in dialogue with each other when there are contentious points. But on the whole we trust each other. We don't have a strict narrative. We might show similar themes, draw parallels, but that will be done lightly. Clips close in theme can even land next to each other in our inbox — and that's where the magic of chance shows itself.</p>

<p><em>Natalia</em>: I put together screenings intuitively — there's no overarching theme, but there can be intersections. I don't have curatorial selection criteria, but I do have some technical tricks. For example, when I'm putting a screening together, I alternate videos by length, so you don't get a run of only long or only short ones. Sometimes you don't notice the echoes and narrative links, and they reveal themselves during the screening! It's as if we're watching the choices of another curator ourselves — the curator of random numbers.</p>

<p><em>Natasha:</em>And here's another important point — videos gathered together work differently on a big screen than on a laptop screen. The scale of the screen gives the visual metaphors in the video a particular monumental character, one you can feel bodily. Screenings like this let a curator present video in an installation format.</p>

<p><strong>How does it all work during a screening?</strong></p>

<p><em>Natalia:</em>We have a strict protocol. Before the screening we assemble a single file lasting between an hour and a half and two hours. The file is built like this: the "Video Art Club" logo, the video's title and the author's name, the video itself, then the logo insert again, the word "Discussion," and then a 6-minute timer appears on screen, moderating the discussion. When the timer starts, we, or the authors — if they're in the room and want to — briefly comment on the work, reading out information about the author, a description of the video. We spend the rest of the time discussing the video with club members.</p>

<p><em>Natasha:</em>We discuss form, contexts, meanings, and try to debunk genre boundaries.</p>

<p><em>Natalia:</em>Because different people come to us — club members often have different experience and context — sometimes a heated discussion breaks out, sometimes there's silence.</p>

<p><em>Natasha:</em>We alternate formats, often improvise — sometimes we ourselves become the instigators of arguments, sometimes we're the only ones discussing. And within these discussions we try to stay non-judgmental and unbiased.</p>

<p><strong>Who is your audience? Are there regulars?</strong></p>

<p><em>Natalia:</em>There are a few regular guests. Right now, because of coronavirus, we have a limit on the number of people in the room, and tickets run out quickly, so we can't precisely outline the audience. There are always authors and their friends at the screenings. We don't aim to make an event for a broad public — we're interested in accumulating a community that works with video. Film directors, clip-makers, ad people, animators, contemporary artists, and people who simply have an interest in capturing life on video and experimenting with a camera's technical possibilities — all of them work with the moving image. When all these people end up in one space, an interesting dialogue can arise among them.</p>

<p><em>Natasha:</em>I'm interested in interacting with a random, uninitiated viewer. It's interesting how someone without specific philosophical or art-historical training perceives everything that's happening. Right now we (the viewer and I) are sizing each other up.</p>

<p><em>Natalia:</em>At the start we were afraid we'd have few people willing to take part. So, as often happens with open-call projects, we invited artist friends to participate: "Hey, you should pay attention, you could take part in this." Some of them later sent us video, but far from all of them. There was one meeting we advertised heavily — the 5th, musical issue. That screening, which we put together as a pre-New Year's party, is a real outlier in the Video Art Club format, because it had no discussion, and because of that it featured 26 videos. We were short on content and wrote to some musician and director friends.</p>

<p><strong>What types of video do you distinguish among what's sent to you?</strong></p>

<p><em>Natalia:</em>On the whole I'm drawn to an analytical approach, cartography and schemas, but I'm not sure it makes sense to carry that interest over onto the Video Art Club. I want to avoid a colonial, paternalistic, or patriarchal gaze. I already feel that we're forcing all these videos to line up one after another, inserting a 6-minute timer. I feel a curatorial power that I want to move away from as much as possible: not instrumentalizing artists by building a frame around them. It seems like cartography and categorization aren't needed here at all. I like the processual archive we're building right now.</p>

<p><em>Natasha:</em>Maybe we'll get to that later. But in any case our processual archive is open to researchers of video art.</p>

<p><em>Natalia:</em>There are a lot of projects devoted to video art with curatorial selection: festivals and exhibitions of experimental film, animation, and video art, like Videoforma, Now&amp;Then; Evgeny Granilshchikov recently ran a video festival at GUM-Red-Line. There are also projects that use similar principles or work with material not labeled as video art: Ilya Grishaev's "Chasclip," Pyotr Zherebtsov's "Echolocation."</p>

<p><strong>How long do you plan to keep doing the screenings?</strong></p>

<p><em>Natasha:</em>We've only just started. As long as there's a flow of material and we still want to. We'll stop when we get tired. The goal is discovery. We're seeing many artists for the first time, some reveal a new side of themselves. I'm forming my own curatorial list, thinking about who I'd like to show in an exhibition format in the future.</p>

<p><em>Natalia:</em>Two aspects of the project interest me: the "event" we create, and the distribution of visibility. I want the participants to get to know each other. Maybe over time the Video Art Club will take on a different form. For example, we talk a lot about how it's a shame our discussions are only accessible locally — in Petersburg. A lot of participants write to us from other cities or countries.</p>

<p><em>Natasha:</em>We're thinking about an online format, but it's not yet clear how to organize and present a screening and discussion in an online broadcast.</p>

<p><strong>Will there be an exhibition based on this material?</strong></p>

<p><em>Natalia:</em>I feel like we already have an exhibition. It's on the Kurekhin Center's website! All the video available to show is there, with wall texts. I don't know whether this material needs to be shown in a gallery or museum space at all. Watching an offline exhibition like that would take 6 hours, by runtime! Does anyone have that much time for a trip to a gallery?</p>

<p><em>Natasha:</em>Plus, an exhibition implies selection, and that contradicts our original idea.</p>

<p><strong>Have you ever shown your own videos at the club?</strong></p>

<p><em>Natalia:</em>I showed one of my own videos at the musical screening, because we were short on clips. Despite the great temptation, I try not to do that. It's always strange when curators appear as artists in their own project. I feel like that's not entirely ethical. If I show my own video, it means I'm taking someone else's place. Of course, as an artist it would be nice for the audience to spend the whole two hours discussing and watching only my videos… But that's a different project :)</p>

<p><em>Natasha:</em>And I don't shoot video! Maybe I'll get there one day. I'm interested in the other roles — viewer and host. Those are different ways of perceiving video. At some point you switch on the viewer in yourself, register your impressions, and then you have to switch sharply and moderate the meeting. It's interesting to watch yourself, to track your own reactions in the process of creating an event. You become a kind of event yourself.</p>

<p><em>Natalia:</em>I've been working with event-based projects for several years now. It used to seem to me that when an awkward silence hangs in the room, that's a failure, you planned something badly. But then I understood that an experimental format also implies a 6-minute silence — and that's worth treating as an experience too. It shouldn't be categorized as "bad" or "good." As part of expanding my own experience, it matters to me, among other things, to step outside the frame and the belief that I must always spend time effectively, and to allow myself to admit chance, mistakes, pauses. We try to maintain accessibility and "watchability" through time limits, keeping screenings to two hours — after all, we also have the Center's closing time. We're not aiming for forced absurdity or transgression — these aren't 12-hour screenings behind closed doors.</p>

<p><em>Natasha:</em>Our format is a living one, we don't mind if people move around during a screening. The Video Art Club is, after all, a performance.</p>
