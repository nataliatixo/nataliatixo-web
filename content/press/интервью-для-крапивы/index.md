<p>(C)</p>

<p><strong>Anastasia Vepreva: Let's start with your first, engineering education. Tell me how you ended up there and what you studied.</strong></p>

<p><strong>Natalia Tikhonova:</strong>I went to school in Dzerzhinsk, in Nizhny Novgorod region. My parents and grandfather were educated in physics/mathematics, and it seemed to me a logical continuation of my schooling. After school I thought about going to study journalism or art, but that felt almost like becoming a cosmonaut — there were no decent humanities universities in Nizhny Novgorod, and I had no examples in front of me of anyone actually working in that field. I knew you could train as a physicist and then still go on to other professions — that kind of education broadens your horizons regardless. I studied at the <a href="http://www.rf.unn.ru/">Faculty of Radiophysics</a>, which is more of an applied field than general theoretical physics. In my first years I even had some interest in doing science, but then I got disillusioned with the university structure itself and moved more into IT, and in my final years I started working as an engineer.<strong>A. V.: But then you decided to study art after all?</strong><strong>N. T.:</strong>In 2010 I came to Petersburg with the idea that I wanted to become an artist. I was interested in contemporary art, but had no idea how to get into that world. So I just started trying different directions: I went to exhibitions, decided to learn to draw. I went to various art schools, signed up for an evening drawing course at the Academy of Arts, took private lessons with a teacher. He's actually the one who gave me the idea to enroll at the <a href="https://ru.wikipedia.org/wiki/%D0%A1%D0%B0%D0%BD%D0%BA%D1%82-%D0%9F%D0%B5%D1%82%D0%B5%D1%80%D0%B1%D1%83%D1%80%D0%B3%D1%81%D0%BA%D0%BE%D0%B5_%D1%85%D1%83%D0%B4%D0%BE%D0%B6%D0%B5%D1%81%D1%82%D0%B2%D0%B5%D0%BD%D0%BD%D0%BE%D0%B5_%D1%83%D1%87%D0%B8%D0%BB%D0%B8%D1%89%D0%B5_%D0%B8%D0%BC%D0%B5%D0%BD%D0%B8_%D0%9D._%D0%9A._%D0%A0%D0%B5%D1%80%D0%B8%D1%85%D0%B0">Roerich Art College</a>. After a year of studying there I realized this wasn't quite what I needed and what interested me. Yes, painting is interesting, but the structure built inside the college is absolutely not contemporary, doesn't resonate with my own worldview specifically, or with contemporary art in general. In my third year I happened to see an open call for the <a href="http://proarte.ru/projects/art/">School for Young Artists at the PRO ARTE Foundation</a>, put together a portfolio of odd works I'd made just for myself with no intention of ever exhibiting them — and by some miracle they took me. There I started immersing myself in the world of contemporary art: what exhibitions exist, what theories, what you could even read. Basically, that's the point when I started doing contemporary art.</p>

<p><br></p>

<p><strong>A. V.: So why did you decide to enroll at Smolny?</strong><strong>N. T.:</strong>After I finished the School for Young Artists and the college, a year went by where I wasn't studying anywhere. I felt like I was rapidly getting dumber and urgently needed to study. I had no humanities education, and I didn't know whether Smolny would take me or whether I could clear the bar of university entrance exams, where you have to write an essay, ideally have a top-grade diploma and experience presenting at academic conferences (meanwhile, participating in exhibitions as a curator or artist formally counts for nothing), so I applied to two master's programs at once — the <a href="https://spbu.ru/postupayushchim/programms/magistratura/prikladnye-fizika-i-matematika">Faculty of Applied Mathematics and Physics</a> and the <a href="https://artesliberales.spbu.ru/ru">Faculty of Liberal Arts and Sciences</a>. I got a state-funded place at Applied Math and Physics, but only a paid place at Smolny. At first I thought I'd study at both faculties, but Applied Math and Physics meant commuting to Peterhof, so I dropped that program and stayed only at Smolny (plus, the stipend from the faculty's endowment fund covered most of the tuition).<strong>A. V.: How do you divide your artistic and curatorial activity for yourself?</strong><strong>N. T.:</strong>I think "curator" is an ambiguous concept, so every curator finds their own definition for themselves. If I call myself a curator, it's always with the footnote that by curator I mean a meta-artist who realizes their ideas in the format of exhibitions, events, spaces, currents, ideas. I know many people might disagree with that definition, and this view of curating can be criticized for the danger of instrumentalizing artists and building power structures inside exhibitions. At the same time, the position of curator-as-mentor doesn't sit with my own inner curatorial ethics. There's a tricky point in combining the curator and artist positions, one that touches on the ethics of relationships within a project. It's important to keep in mind that curator and artist speak from different positions. When I started doing curatorial projects, that became a logical continuation of my artistic activity, which I try to steer toward collaboration and cooperation. Right now what interests me most is the very reproduction of events and optics whose realizations I feel are missing — for example, online platforms or media, where there may be no artists at all, only strategies. And at that point it's unclear whether this is even curating anymore — maybe it's art management. Organizers like that are also artists and actors of cultural production. In the future, I think, ideally all these roles will collapse into the simple definition of "actor."<strong>A. V.: An art worker.</strong>N. T.: Yes.<strong>A. V.: Let's set the curator aside for a moment and talk about the artist — what is that, and what's their ethics?</strong>N. T.: I agree with the position that an artist is someone who positions themselves as one. The definition of "artist" comes from self-identification. It mattered to me to go through schools and institutions in order, as it seemed to me, to gain a legitimate right to call myself an artist — as if that required some external frame or approval. It's interesting: it turns out that when you go through those institutions, you yourself decide how to use that frame in relation to yourself. It's in that freedom of self-identification that your "artist-ness" gets laid down, so to speak.</p>

<p><strong>A. V.: And the ethics?</strong>N. T.: I liked a phrase, though I don't remember who said it — that ethics should be very simple, while aesthetics can be complex. And a related phrase, that ethics is the new aesthetics. If you combine these two statements, which are opposite in meaning, the truth lies somewhere between them. Often, for instance in art or in politics, people start flirting with ethics: sure, you can't kill, but if you really have to, just a little, then maybe it's fine? Or is violence permissible in some cases? It seems to me that all this flirting with ethics is the wrong game. One way or another, for everyone, ethics should be describable in a few simple phrases. For example: you can't kill, you can't steal — everything that can be formulated in the format of commandments. And all of that should hold for the artist and for the president alike — for anyone. Whereas aesthetics can be complex, and can illuminate the nuances of ethical questions.<strong>A. V.: You had a multilayered research project — "The Blue Series" — where you worked with different types of memory. Tell me about it.</strong>N. T.: It started with <a href="https://www.goethe.de/ins/ru/ru/kul/arc/2017/blockade.html">research</a> launched by the Goethe-Institut about possible monuments dedicated to the Siege of Leningrad. The project lasted two years and ended with an <a href="https://www.kunstverein.de/ausstellungen/rueckblick/900-und-etwa-26000-tage">exhibition</a> in Hamburg, then at the <a href="https://www.goethe.de/ins/ru/ru/kul/arc/2017/blockade/20936757.html?forceDesktop=1">New Museum</a> in Petersburg, and it also became part of the project "<a href="https://www.spbmuseum.ru/exhibits_and_exhibitions/93/49718/">Quiet Voices</a>." For the project I looked for statistical data on the Siege — for example, how many people actually died, since very different figures appear in different sources.</p>

<p>While working with documents about the Siege, I came across <a href="https://spiral.lynn.edu/specialcollections/2/">materials</a> from the Nuremberg Trials, which I think became an important rupture, a point in time when some decisive shifts in human history took place. It matters that at the trial both sides were heard — both the prosecution and the accused. The tribunal was conducted in several languages, including Russian, but it was never published in Russian in full — there's a <a href="http://militera.lib.ru/docs/da/np8/index.html">text</a> in 8 volumes (the full transcript of the process runs to 42 volumes), containing an extract of the part of the story that was convenient for the USSR. This case seemed interesting to me as an attempt to represent memory. The project "The Blue Series" consists of several parts. In one part I reproduced all 42 volumes of the Trial as an artifact, together with Surik Asaturyan and Liza Mzhagina, restorers from the Hermitage. They helped me make a binding with Russian lettering on the cover, as if these were the Nuremberg Trials volumes in Russian.<br></p>

<p><strong>A. V.: And what was inside the binding?</strong><strong>N. T.:</strong>There were blank pages inside; I made the books based on the Concise Soviet Encyclopedia, but that's not so important — what mattered more to me was to represent the sheer mass of text of those 42 volumes, since this whole story is not only a moral but also a physically heavy state to sit with. To make the reading easier, I started using different text-processing programs. That echoed my earlier works, like "<a href="/projects/no-words-no-war/">No Words No War</a>," where a person tries, so to speak, to build additional borders around themselves in order not to take in traumatic events. Here I offload the weight of reading and analysis onto a program. Out of these experiments came many hours of video in the manner of a video performance — for example, the program searching for the word "Leningrad" in the text, counting how many times it repeats, or reading numbers aloud. I also used a "text-to-speech" program to turn the text into audio, and recorded the whole process onto discs, so these audio files could be listened to anywhere, for instance in the car on the way to and from work. It was an experiment in folding memory into everyday life. And after some more time I made the third part of the project: by then the books had already been <a href="https://mira1.ru/feed/1470">to an exhibition</a> in Krasnoyarsk, then they sat in the studio for a long time, I kept dragging them from one apartment to another — genuine physical labor. I decided it wasn't right to keep everything myself and started passing the books on to friends and acquaintances, to build a cartography of the handing-over and keeping of these books, and also so it would stop weighing so heavily on my life. Incidentally, you can still borrow the books from me now. The next step could be a collective translation of the trial materials and a publication, but I haven't yet found the resources in myself to take that on. But I feel that once the full Russian text of the Nuremberg Tribunal is published, something might change on a global scale. That's an important process that, in our history, we never completed.</p>

<p><strong>A. V.: Going back to curating — you have a number of projects built on relational aesthetics — the Babushkas project and the "Procrastinator Brunch." Tell me more about them — how did they come about?</strong><strong>N. T.:</strong>If we're talking about Babushkas, I insist that it's a project about projects in the relational-aesthetics genre, because it tries to keep its distance from them. There's a conflict there, a certain kind of manipulation present in every project of that genre, and I try not to hide it but to problematize it. I was interested in how contemporary art works in the post-Soviet space, and I got the idea to discuss relational-aesthetics work and socially engaged art with people who grew up in the USSR. I found it an interesting analogy that in the USSR, art was also perceived as "useful," as something functional that had to be socially meaningful. I came to the "Comprehensive Center for Social Services of the Petrogradsky District" at 30 Chkalovsky Prospect together with Lera Lerner, who helped me negotiate with the organizers, and I proposed doing a series of conversations with the center's visitors, folding it into their summer program. We held a series of lecture-conversation-tea-parties, where I talked about projects of socially engaged and politically committed art, and afterward we discussed the methods and issues of such art in general. Among the projects we discussed were, for example, projects by Jeremy Deller, Tania Bruguera, Arkhstoyanie, and others. After these conversations we made a <a href="https://ru.nataliatixo.com/babushka-s">zine and a video.</a></p>

<p><strong>A. V.: And why do you say there's none of the manipulation here that's usually present in such projects? It often happens that an artist draws people into their artistic process, and it doesn't really matter what exactly they do there, but it all ends up framed as the author's own artistic product.</strong><strong>N. T.:</strong>There is manipulation here, but it's also the very subject of discussion. From the start I said I'd come to talk about art and to record our conversations on video and in interview format. I didn't say this project would have social significance. I was interested in making art about art — that position was stated from the outset. I think what matters here is precisely the transparency of the relationship. Yes, there was video recording during the meetings, we had lavalier mics — that also raised a certain degree of intensity, because many of the round-table participants partly theatricalized their remarks. They specifically shaped their speech for the camera, giving it a kind of recitation.<strong>A. V.: That's an important difference from the "Procrastinator Brunch," where you kept no documentation at all.</strong><strong>N. T.:</strong>In form the two projects are similar — both end up as a brunch. I carried Babushkas around in my head and prepared it for three years, and even submitted it to Nastya Kizilova's <a href="https://www.foundproject.net/">Found Project</a>, a database of ideas for artists — and based on it, a lot of schools of contemporary art for pensioners sprang up across Russia (<em>laughs</em>). But the "<a href="https://procartistination.timepad.ru/">Procrastinator Brunch</a>" I just started doing right away, out of my own need for that kind of practice, and only explained the reasons to myself afterward. These are also meetings, for which I make presentations based on texts, articles, clips, conversations and comments online, informational noise, and then we discuss them in the format of an art brunch. It turns out that the only documentation of the meetings is the presentation files themselves. Recently, in a research <a href="https://garagemca.org/ru/event/workshop-how-heroes-left-and-we-stayed-actionism-2020">group</a> on actionism and activism at Garage Museum, we were discussing exactly this — how to document a large volume of comments, media noise, media actions, and clips. And these brunches are trying to grab hold of the structure of feeling, the language of events, usually not captured by media and researchers. If ideas for further discussion come up during a meeting, I add them to the presentation afterward. The absence of video and audio documentation is an important part of the project. It's a kind of safe space, or even a psychotherapeutic practice, where you can share some rumor or experience off the record.<strong>A. V.: So it turns out you're making a kind of digest of events.</strong><strong>N. T.:</strong>Yes, that can include events, or even a comment on Facebook — since that's also an important part of the media field.</p>

<p><strong>A. V.: In your project for the Ural Industrial Biennial, "</strong><a href="http://fourth.uralbiennale.ru/places/parallel-program/communication-management-unit"><strong>Communication Management Unit</strong></a><strong>," you build a meta-superstructure by bringing together artists who work with relational aesthetics. Tell me how you managed to get into the biennial at all, and what the logistics of this project were like.</strong><strong>N. T.:</strong>I'd had the idea for a long time to make an exhibition in the format of a call center. Of course there were precedents — for example Francis Alÿs did the project "<a href="https://www.castellodirivoli.org/en/mostra/francis-alys-matrix-2/">Matrix II</a>" back in 2002. And of course there's a tradition of <a href="https://ru.wikipedia.org/wiki/%D0%A2%D0%B5%D0%BB%D0%B5%D0%BC%D0%B0%D1%82%D0%B8%D1%87%D0%B5%D1%81%D0%BA%D0%BE%D0%B5_%D0%B8%D1%81%D0%BA%D1%83%D1%81%D1%81%D1%82%D0%B2%D0%BE">telematic art</a>. I couldn't understand why no one had made a proper exhibition in call-center format yet? I kept suggesting to everyone that they realize this idea, and people would say, "Oh, great, go do it." Myself (<em>laughs</em>). First and foremost, I was interested in the democratic quality and inclusivity that this technology offers — anyone calling that number could become a participant in the exhibition, artists and "viewers" from different cities and social positions alike. On the other hand, this was also where I formulated my thoughts on exactly what bothers me about projects involving audience interaction — the impossibility of easily leaving them. For example, as a viewer you come to a lecture or a round table, and if you realize what's happening isn't to your liking, isn't interesting, you can't easily step out of that project. That element of being held in place carries a certain degree of violence and sets up a particular kind of power for the artist. But if we put such projects into a call-center format, that can be avoided, because the listener can hang up the phone at any moment. So I saw an open call from the Ural Biennial for its parallel program and sent this project there. And they took me. I invited artist friends and also ran an additional open call. In essence, I was inviting artists to join in on this kind of scheme and turn their work into an audio file, which required a certain level of trust in me on their part. We made audio recordings for seven different projects, uploaded them to a server, connected them to a phone number, which was also printed in the Ural Biennial's catalog. In the end we got around 600 calls. The number kept working for about another two years, but it doesn't exist anymore now.<strong>A. V.: Was there any feedback? Or is it unknown who listened all the way through and who didn't?</strong><strong>N. T.:</strong>When someone called, they landed in a <a href="https://youtu.be/eWZcajJV0DY">menu</a>. They could go into the exhibition and choose one of seven artists, they could listen to the curatorial text, or they could leave a review. There were three or four reviews total, but I don't remember anything remarkable in them. There was, for instance, the project by Nastya Kizilova I already mentioned — Found Project — where you could listen to projects already in her database, or leave your own. Many of the projects assumed viewers would leave something behind, but it seems like no one ever did.</p>

<p><strong>A. V.: Did the Biennial pay for this? Was there a budget?</strong><strong>N. T.:</strong>No, there was nothing.<strong>A. V.: So where do you get money for your projects?</strong><strong>N. T.:</strong>I work (<em>laughs</em>).<strong>A. V.: Does paid work take up a lot of time, and how much is actually left for art?</strong><strong>N. T.:</strong>I work remotely, and it's usually eight hours a day, but I work an evening schedule, on US time. So I have a day free to do my own art projects, then I work as an engineer, and then there's also the night to make art projects.<strong>A. V.: And sleep?</strong><strong>N. T.:</strong>Sleep is for the weak.<strong>A. V.: Fair enough.</strong><strong>N. T.:</strong>But sometimes I get grants or fees. For example, I made Babushkas on a fee from CEC ArtsLink as part of an Art Prospect <a href="https://www.artprospect.org/babushkas">residency</a> — they reached out to me themselves with a proposal to realize this project. Honestly, this is a sore subject, I could go on about it for a long time. People often tell me it's wrong that I make almost all my projects with my own money, and that I should find ways to get funding, but my experience trying to find outside budgets for projects has mostly been negative. The work you have to do to get a grant or funding from a cultural institution in Russia usually far exceeds the work you'd have to do to just earn that money yourself. The hierarchies and lack of transparency in relationships like that produce feelings in me ranging from awkwardness and discomfort all the way to shame. And on top of that they place obligations and restrictions on you, they grade you, you feel dependent. But I'd like to find approaches that actually work for me. For example, right now we're finding out how to get grants for large projects if you register as an NGO. That's probably the only possibility for systemic work in Russia today on one's own terms.<strong>A. V.: I recently had an argument with a painter about how ecological technology-based art is. He said painting is far more ecological than, say, video, since the latter uses a huge amount of electricity when it runs around the clock for days on end in an exhibition. And actually a lot of things that seem immaterial carry a big ecological footprint — for example, all data is stored on servers that also constantly consume energy, even ordinary emails aren't ecological in that sense. And you've had two projects —</strong><a href="https://www.facebook.com/groups/285927725292534/"><strong>Art Garbage Community</strong></a><strong>, a platform for redistributing art materials, and not long ago there was the radio "</strong><a href="https://www.facebook.com/Hopesanddreamsradio/"><strong>Hopes and Dreams</strong></a><strong>" at Garage, which addressed environmental issues directly. So what do you think about all this?</strong><strong>N. T.:</strong>I think everything connected with painting and installation, any object-based art on the theme of ecology, is to some extent a fraud. That whole rhetoric just isn't what should be at work when we talk about ecology. Ecology is, first and foremost, a political subject, and projects like that should contain direct political statements. We can argue endlessly about what's more ecological — painting or technological art — while meanwhile Norilsk Nickel is simply dumping its waste into the environment. So if I, as an artist, speak on the subject of ecology, that statement has to be as open, political, and concrete as possible. This is a real problem for people who go out to protests or defend Shiyes. If you make a project about ecology in our context while talking about Timothy Morton and steering the conversation toward aestheticization and "philosophizing," that amounts to a pretty brutal manipulation. I think that when talking about ecological issues in Russia, first and foremost it's important to talk about political activists, about ecological projects that work systemically. The main ecological problem in Russia today isn't that people don't know about this or that situation, and it isn't about individual habits — it's the absence of functioning democratic institutions.As for the "Radio Hopes and Dreams" project, this is a <a href="https://garagemca.org/ru/event/faculty-of-elin-m-r-yen-vister-radio-hopes-and-dreams">project</a> by artist Elin Már Øyen Vister from Norway. She was invited to take part in the exhibition "<a href="https://thecomingworld.garagemca.org/">The Coming World</a>" at Garage Museum, but she felt it was wrong for an exhibition about ecology to have world-famous artists flying in on planes, bringing big works, without engaging with the local agenda. So she initiated the radio project, which had earlier been realized in Norway.To put together the program for the Moscow version she invited three artists — Lyolya Nordic, Diana Burkot, and me. We spent a long time thinking about which problems we could fold into it. In the end, the two-day radio <a href="https://www.facebook.com/events/431092444424696/">program</a>, alongside sound art and performances by artists, included conferences and dialogues with activists, live connections with NGOs, conversations with Arkhangelsk, and some people came to our studio in person — for example, Irina Kozlovskikh from Greenpeace. Activist <a href="https://novayagazeta.ru/news/2019/02/02/148910-organizatora-protestov-protiv-musornogo-poligona-ryadom-s-kolomnoy-otpravili-pod-domashniy-arest">Vyacheslav Egorov</a>, who works on the issue of waste-incineration plant construction near Kolomna, was at that very moment under investigation for repeatedly violating rules on holding protests, was under house arrest with a ban on using the internet — we reached him by phone so he could tell his little-known story.On the whole I liked how it all came together, but I'm unhappy that the project had few viewers and no good PR, that it didn't go beyond an ordinary museum event. I'd have liked it to.Art Garbage Coop is my own reflections on the topic of systemically dealing with the toxicity of art production and reducing its eco-footprint. It's a group for redistributing materials and equipment left over after exhibitions. I'm also currently occupied with the connection between military technology and ecology, which I started developing at the Suoja/Shelter festival in Helsinki, in the project "<a href="https://youtu.be/Z-rHuAossk4">Technologies of Presence</a>." Military technologies are often connected with marking territory, and at the same time, one of the markers of human presence is garbage — I want to somehow cross militarism with sustainable-development theory.</p>

<p><strong>A. V.: But isn't it more effective to address a given problem from several sides at once, since everyone has different channels of perception, and it's unclear whether a direct statement or a strong metaphor, an image that defamiliarizes seemingly ordinary things, will work better? Do you still have recordings from the radio?</strong><strong>N. T.:</strong>After it ended, Elin Már left for Norway by train, and we didn't have the resources to gather the recordings and post them anywhere. But yes, I'm interested in art not so much as a call to action, but as a guide to it. Like in activism, where you have your so-called "headliners," who work as a megaphone attracting attention, but alongside them there's a whole mass of activists doing systemic work. And it only works as a combination, because shouting on its own, apart from a psychotherapeutic effect, doesn't lead anywhere. Whereas in art, more often than not, there's only the shouting. For example, if an artist makes a project about some endangered animal species, it wouldn't hurt to do it in cooperation with an NGO that's been working on this for ten years already and also has something to say about it — everyone benefits from that. Though of course I don't want to dictate to anyone what the "right" or "wrong" artistic method is.<strong>A. V.: Continuing this line of databases and practical action, it would be really interesting to hear about the "</strong><a href="https://spbart.atlassian.net/wiki"><strong>Spravochnik</strong></a><strong>" ("Handbook") project, which was created specifically to gather genuinely practical knowledge for art workers.</strong><strong>N. T.:</strong>"Spravochnik" is a joint project with Katya Sokolovskaya and Tanya Kiryanova, which we do in parallel with our "<a href="https://www.youtube.com/channel/UC-9nZYvQSWuyHD_dFCV5pfQ">Natural Circulation</a>" meetings, supported by the State Center for Contemporary Art (GTsSI) in Petersburg. It came out of our reflections: here in Petersburg we have GTsSI, and very artist-friendly people work there, but they have few resources. GTsSI has an office and symbolic capital, but they can't share money on spontaneous projects today, since all their funding exists for specific, designated projects. So we came up with the idea of holding meetings for artists that could take place in their cozy office. Not lectures, but meetings with practical goals, which could lead to collaborations, actions, changes, new acquaintances. Many people asked why we needed institutional support at all — well, exactly to make use of GTsSI's symbolic capital and resources to draw in a large number of interested people and speakers. We gathered a first "test" working group to figure out what the theme and general agenda of these meetings could be, what could be talked about. About forty people from the most varied fields showed up. We asked those who came what they were interested in talking about, and the proposals were wildly polarized: let's form a union, let's set up a foundation, or just a café where we gather. But those were all separate, individual voices, and we spent another two weeks or so figuring out how to bring it together, and put forward two projects for discussion (<em>laughs</em>). I still dream about the first one happening someday — an off-site camp for organizers and curators from various Petersburg institutions, for team-building or family therapy somewhere in the woods. So that all the initiators, organizers, and heads of cultural institutions — from Rosa's House of Culture and Fotodepartament to the Russian Museum, the Hermitage, and the Manege — would all go through family therapy and agree on joint action. But we decided we didn't have the funding for that yet. The second idea was to make an Encyclopedia — a Handbook, half-practical, half-utopian, and to build subsequent meetings around it. And then the pandemic started, the meetings moved online, and it all came together into a more coherent story — an online handbook and online meetings. Right now the Handbook has open moderation, meaning anyone can contribute something to it. It's also an experiment in archiving — it's a convenient form, like Wikipedia, fairly neutral and universal, and aimed specifically at the professional community, which is capable of putting in the extra effort to dig around in it. For us, what mattered most was the practical value of the knowledge — for example, if we're talking about setting up a union, then let's bring in lawyers who can explain how a foundation differs from an NGO and a union, what steps you need to take for that, and what problems might come up. That's where we start, rather than with talk about what's already been said and theorized about unions, or what kinds of unions exist in Europe, without accounting for our own context today. Philosophical reflection and analysis are of course also very important, but right now I think the problem is more about practice than philosophy. For me, "Spravochnik" is more media activism, as a practice of working in the field.</p>

<p><strong>A. V.: And is there a workshop for collaborative work on texts within this context, or is that a separate process?</strong><strong>N. T.:</strong>That's my own separate project, same as my <a href="https://t.me/procartistination">Telegram channel</a> — that one's more about my own need to work with text. "<a href="mailto:patthetext@yandex.ru">Pat the Text</a>" is an anonymous writing-practice circle, where you can send any text of yours to an email address to be commented on and edited by other participants. Usually five or six authors are involved in one batch. Texts get redistributed among the participants, and this practice of circular editing happens.<strong>A. V.: And is there some methodology for the editing, or does everyone just do whatever they want?</strong><strong>N. T.:</strong>Whatever they want. Nobody knows each other. You might send in a project description for editing, and someone might criticize the project itself, while another will act like a copy editor and only fix the text or the punctuation. And when you hand over your text, you understand that it might not be professional editors doing it, you don't know exactly who's editing you, and you might not agree with that editing.<strong>A. V.: Why do you think artists in general don't write very well or very often, don't talk much, and there's something of a crisis of writing about contemporary art? A lot of people do things, but because of the lack of speech, of analysis, it all just disappears.</strong><strong>N. T.:</strong>I think that's really not an artists' problem, but a problem with the whole of Russian education. I talked about this with my friend Dasha, who's a linguist and teaches languages, and it turns out that when students come to her to prepare for the TOEFL, it becomes clear that many of them can't write an essay not just in English but in Russian either — we don't have that school of speaking and argumentation. So of course artists also have trouble constructing speech, but I wouldn't say there's a crisis of writing — quite the opposite, there's a lot of it right now: on Syg.ma, on Spectate, in Khudozhestvenny Zhurnal, on aroundart, in Telegram channels, so many texts, I can't keep up reading them all. I only recently arrived at the idea myself that you need to describe everything you do. But there's often no resources for that. You're working — you're your own artist, curator, and SMM manager all at once, and on top of that you also have to describe and document in detail everything you've done — that takes a lot of time and energy. But maybe it's different for other artists.<strong>A. V.: Many people think their art speaks for itself. And I've also often heard the opinion that when an artist describes themselves too well, there's nothing left for a critic to do, and as a result no one writes about them except themselves.</strong><strong>N. T.:</strong>I don't really understand that kind of practice. When we started working with texts, an artist wrote to me asking whether I could recommend someone to describe her work for her — not a curatorial text, but literally to describe the meaning of the project, not even to edit it. And I know a lot of artists do this. And I find that amazing, because in essence that's already co-authorship. And we also don't have that many contemporary-art schools, and even in the ones that exist, attention is rarely paid to the practice of writing, to what's called "critical writing." Although at Smolny there was actually something like that, a course on art criticism. But in the contemporary-art schools I don't remember any courses like that, and that's very strange. For example, at the Academy of Fine Arts in Helsinki, as far as I know, an essay is part of defending your art project.<strong>A. V.: You recently wrote a </strong><a href="https://syg.ma/@natalia-tikhonova/onlain-platformy-kak-ghibridnyie-formy-vystavok-vstuplieniie-pro-uslovnost"><strong>text</strong></a><strong> on Syg.ma about online platforms, a sore subject of the recent quarantine months, when everything that could moved online, and a lot of people, including me, started climbing the walls because of it. You talk there about the "special theory of relativity of the exhibition" — could you say more about what that means? And there's also an interesting statement, that in your view artists "understand the political potential of technology" and "aren't afraid of being destroyed by it" — I can both agree and disagree there, depending on how you define this technology, since a lot of people still don't understand exactly how various things work, and saying that everyone feels at ease in the technological field seems a bit reckless to me.</strong><strong>N. T.:</strong>This text is part of my master's thesis at Smolny, and I'm planning to still finish it. It's coming very hard for me, because everything keeps changing, and I keep wanting to rewrite it. I started writing this text back in 2016, when I began work on Communication Management Unit. What annoyed me was that there's a large number of exhibitions and competitions on the theme of technology, where, for the most part, technology is used as an attraction to grab attention. A vivid example is the <a href="https://hermitagemuseum.org/wps/portal/hermitage/what-s-on/temp_exh/2019/innovation/!ut/p/z1/pVPJcqMwFPyWOXAEPcQm5kawyyuFs2AbLi6MBWjKEg5WTPL3IzI-jLM4NRNOoqpb3a9fC2VojTKRn1iVS9aIfK_-08zdxEHgmlYI03gy8yCIzWQ48SZ4MfHQ6hUAn3wBoOySv1B8ZzTw7rwQRkN85l8BZNf1lyhD2V5UKG2f-uOhYDuUFoXr5MXO1y2yo7pt-UT3Se7qGAoPE8ffOaXdowshD7JGaU1bzmReUQ2KRkgqpAb0uWZb1udw1EBSfmjavH3RAIPpa8CEaE6vKb0f4a3H7HpCq97JxQ0kIBCMnEUUJXMcz6x3gD8SD844jpchDu0z4IrIvYonVUa9zRxMd-zb5pQ44wEEeDkLx7Zngq-WcWK0Q4loWq52f_-PeY7hrPBZFLfwkcIqjDa3yfBOcf9vj1-MZX5zrOlXFVRvBLdRGFXq2lzWOhNlg9Z9TdD6r5ooGPv1-JgFqnZ9x54lWn-rd8oY23KjK7gBhoMdMC0fgHjYs4nbywViaxHlqqUlbWlrPLXqQddSHo4_NdCg6zqjappqT42i4Rp8RKmbo7J5iUQHniScWFxfA1vUFd8MbiK6Jd1DyVeLeRlJJ_3xGwRHzhY!/dz/d5/L2dBISEvZ0FBIS9nQSEh/?lng=ru">exhibition</a> about Artificial Intelligence at the Hermitage, which featured a lot of artists working with AI as a technique for generating new visual material. It's a similar situation to the one with ecology — artists aestheticize and instrumentalize, but don't want to talk about the political problems hidden behind the development of technology and its political potential. And even though I'm an engineer and generally on first-name terms with technology, when I make projects on the theme of technology, in the optics of contemporary art many people don't see that these are actually very technological projects. And when I build an online platform, a "Handbook" for artists, that's precisely a project about technology, about how Wikipedia and the processes of democratizing knowledge can work in the field of art. Sure, you can make big installations and show plasma screens with video where flowers turn into pixels, but if we're talking about keeping a critical distance from technology, it's important to talk about the socio-political significance of AI, data-analysis technologies, or distributed networks.And the "special theory of relativity of the exhibition" is the result of my attempt to theorize exhibition practices in the format of online platforms, which I researched in my master's thesis at Smolny. Many of the professors at Smolny talked about online platforms, about websites, as a subordinate medium, and in my view that's a very strange optic. I think online exhibitions need to be looked at from the position of social sculpture, performative theory, the archival turn, or from the position of "<a href="https://ru.wikipedia.org/wiki/%D0%9A%D0%BE%D1%80%D0%BF%D1%83%D1%81%D0%BA%D1%83%D0%BB%D1%8F%D1%80%D0%BD%D0%BE-%D0%B2%D0%BE%D0%BB%D0%BD%D0%BE%D0%B2%D0%BE%D0%B9_%D0%B4%D1%83%D0%B0%D0%BB%D0%B8%D0%B7%D0%BC">wave-particle duality</a>," where an object can take on the properties of both a particle and a wave. Then the online platform becomes a continuation of the informational wave that accompanies any art event, and in some cases that wave is itself the basis of the artwork, shifting attention completely away from the object. There can be many examples here: from the Situationists to Actionists, where documentation is often the main body of the work itself. As for concerns — during the pandemic a lot of people again said that online can't replace a "real" exhibition in a museum. Reasoning like that rests on a dichotomy between "online" and "offline," as if some separation were even possible between a museum and its digital archive. This dichotomy is most often reproduced by those who feel insecure in the online environment and are afraid that one day they won't be able to understand a message in a language foreign to them. But if you grew up in a bilingual environment, like digital natives, for instance, then, as a bilingual person, you live in one shared symbolic field, and you automatically switch between modes. It's no longer possible to imagine yourself existing in only one or the other linguistic environment, and one doesn't contradict the other. Technology doesn't destroy the museum, but rather redefines it, transforms it, the way photography redefined painting, and cinema redefined theater — the same thing is happening now with exhibitions.Further on in my thesis I describe and classify the different formats in which art can work with online platforms — as a search for a new visual language, as documentation, as gallery fiction, as performance, as event, as archive, as database, as educational projects, as media activism.<strong>A. V.: But are exhibitions even necessary at all?</strong><strong>N. T.:</strong>I think about that a lot right now. There's a big question in there — what an exhibition even is. If we're talking about an event in geographic space, where some kind of frame gets set — then exhibitions like that are probably necessary, as a focus and a point of reference. It seems to me that the important exhibitions today are exactly the ones trying to answer that question — why do we make them at all?</p>

<p><strong>A. V.: You did the project N+1 rooms at the Nepokorennykh studio</strong><a href="https://www.nplusonerooms.info/"><strong></strong></a><strong>, as an experiment aimed at "redefining the concepts of space and exhibition form in the digital era."</strong><strong>N. T.:</strong>That ran in parallel with my thesis, as part of its defense. There I build a triangle between the viewer, the artist, and the curator/gallery space that initiates that connection. I invited artists from different cities to take part in the project and sent them surveillance cameras. The artists themselves decided what they wanted to broadcast. The starting point was the room as a space. Everyone made their own works, and for 10 days a broadcast ran on screens in the gallery and on the website.It's funny that back then a lot of people said, well, who's going to sit and watch an exhibition on a screen — a year before the pandemic. Some of the artists broadcast events or objects, and some broadcast a screen on which, for example, a video was playing. So we ended up folding everything onto the plane of the screen, and it was interesting to watch how these relationships worked. In my case the space of the exhibition was exactly that triangle — the space in between. At the same time, it mattered to me to keep the gallery part of the exhibition too, where the screens with broadcasts worked at scale, lectures and tours were held, and you could meet the project's team. I also did a performance about redefining the time and place of the exhibition, "On Line," at the Kurekhin Center, at the <a href="https://projectimpulse.wixsite.com/exhibition/events">exhibition</a> "Impulse," where, conversely, the projects existed within the center's walls but were arranged along a timeline. During the performance I led a mediation through the exhibition, where instead of wall texts I had a timer — for each of the 12 projects I set a viewing time, say 40 seconds, 2 minutes, 3 minutes, 30 seconds. We'd walk up to a work, I'd set the timer and say nothing. People would look it over, then move on to the next one and view it on a timer too. That way the exhibition turned into a single timeline.When you make an exhibition on a website, you work with time differently. When you come to a museum for an hour, you have to somehow distribute your time across the works, but on a website the viewer can come back for five minutes every day.You could say that's exactly the core question for artists and curators — in answering why they're making an exhibition, why they need this event within the walls of a gallery or on a website, and what will happen there, they define their strategies, visuality, the temporality of their interaction with the viewer, ultimately their own artistic language. During the pandemic, everyone started remembering how bad things are without exhibitions. It created a sense that there's no other form besides exhibitions. But as an artist you can make clips for YouTube, post visual material on social media — why are you interested specifically in working in the genre of an indoor exhibition? It turns out that a lot of processes don't need to be fixed in that form at all. I just like making exhibitions (<em>laughs</em>). I like working with space the way you'd work with a theatrical one.</p>

<p><strong>A. V.: Exhibitions are also needed for socializing. That's an interesting field for research. I'd like to write something on this topic. Why are exhibitions necessary? I have a few theories on that. An exhibition can work like weeping, like a therapy session, like a school, like a circus, like a church.</p>
